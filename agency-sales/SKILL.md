## 1. THE SALES PHILOSOPHY — The "Why We Win"

### 1.1 What This Group Is (and How to State It)

That Pepper is not a marketing agency. It is a strategic partner that sits at the client's table and thinks their business with them. Never receive a brief and execute — understand the business, the commercial objectives, and the strategy before proposing anything.

Display is not a production company. It is the infrastructure that makes the agency's promises executable without friction. The studio is not a rental space. It is a controlled environment that gives the agency capabilities no standalone agency can match.

**The integrated ecosystem argument — use it in every proposal:**
The studio competes against every studio in Buenos Aires, but having a production company makes the studio stop competing alone. The production company competes against other production companies, but having an agency gives it value no conventional production company can offer. The agency completes the system with strategy, creativity, and measurable commercial results. The differentiator is not having more services — it is that the services talk to each other. The agency knows what to demand from production. Production knows what the agency needs. That does not exist anywhere else.

### 1.2 What the Group Does Not Do — and How to Handle It

State limitations as positioning, not as failure. Honesty generates more trust than over-promising.

| Service | Position |
|---|---|
| Traditional media buying (TV, OOH) | "We don't execute the buy, but we build the media strategy and can coordinate with a media agency. Our proposal includes a complete digital media plan." |
| PR / press management | "We can include it when it serves a launch or event we're already executing. For complex ongoing PR needs, we recommend complementing with a specialist." |
| Paid media buying (traditional) | "We focus on digital. We recommend who covers the rest, and we offer to sit in meetings with the client's paid team to align messaging and media." |

Never say "we can't do that." Redirect: identify what is covered, what needs a partner, and offer to coordinate.

## 2. PRE-MEETING PROTOCOL — Before Every Sales Conversation

Before entering any prospecting or proposal meeting, complete this sequence. Do not skip steps.

### 2.1 Prospect Qualification

Identify the following before the meeting. If the information is not available, these become the first questions:

1. **Why now?** What changed that made them look for an agency at this moment? Did they come from another agency? What worked and what didn't?
2. **What is the real objective?** Awareness, sales, positioning, launch, or rebranding? Distinguish between what they say and what the business actually needs.
3. **Who decides?** Map the decision chain. Who approves budget? Who approves creative? Are they the same person?
4. **What is already resolved?** What is covered by another provider or internal team? What do they specifically need from an external partner?
5. **What is the working dynamic?** Monthly fee or per-project? How do they measure success? What are their timelines and urgency levels?
6. **Budget range?** Ask directly or offer to show options at different price points. Never build a proposal without a budget signal.

### 2.2 Identify the Client Type Before the Meeting

Match the prospect to one of the seven client types in Section 5. The client type determines the approach, the proposal format, and the case study to lead with.

### 2.3 Prepare the Personal Connection

Before the meeting, identify one genuine point of personal connection — not a business connection. A shared category interest, a mutual contact, a relevant personal experience. This converts the first minutes from a pitch into a conversation between peers.

## 3. THE CONVERSATION FLOW — How to Run a Sales Meeting

Execute this sequence in order. Never open with the agency. Never lead with the portfolio.

### Step 1 — Listen First (First 10–15 minutes)
Let the client talk. Ask the qualifying questions from Section 2.1. Intervene with clarifying questions, not with pitch. The goal of this phase is to hear the client say their problem in their own words.

### Step 2 — Diagnose Out Loud
Before proposing anything, verbalize your reading of the situation. Name the real problem — not the stated request. Connect what they said to the business implication.
> Example: "Lo que escucho es que la marca tiene un problema de percepción, no de visibilidad. No es que nadie la conoce — es que quienes la conocen no la eligen. Eso es un relanzamiento, no un manejo de redes."

If the diagnosis is right, the client will confirm it. If it is partially right, they will correct it. Either way, you demonstrate you understood the business, not just the brief.

### Step 3 — Introduce the Agency Organically
Never introduce the agency first. Introduce it at the moment when it naturally solves the diagnosed problem. Lucas typically opens a meeting explaining why Flor is in the room; Flor enters after listening to the full need. Replicate this sequence: establish the problem, then position the solution.

### Step 4 — Propose Options, Never One Thing
Always offer a minimum of 2 options with different scope and price. The options must make the difference in value tangible — not just the difference in price. Never present a single budget. The client must be able to choose, not just accept or reject.

### Step 5 — Do Not Compete on Price
When a client compares to other agencies or pushes back on cost: do not lower the price. Differentiate by the integrated ecosystem. "No hay otra agencia que tenga productora, estudio y agencia bajo el mismo techo." If scope needs to be reduced to match a budget, reduce scope explicitly — never margin silently.

## 4. PROPOSAL FORMATS — Match the Format to the Client

Select the proposal format based on the client type and need. Never use a format that is more complex than the situation requires. Never use a format that is too simple for a strategic client.

### Format A — Strategic Integral Proposal
**Use when:** The client needs a continuous strategic communication partner. Clients: Personal SMB, Personal Tech, Champion, Under Armour.

**Sequence (mandatory, in this order):**
1. **Context / Insight** — Diagnosis of the client's current moment. Demonstrate you understood the business before you understood the brief.
2. **Strategic Concept** — One central creative idea that governs all communication. One headline. One insight that justifies it.
3. **Communication Pillars** — 3–4 thematic axes, each with a specific function. Name them. Do not list topics — define roles.
4. **Platform Role** — What LinkedIn does, what Instagram does, what TikTok does. Each platform has one function. Platforms are not interchangeable.
5. **Content Proposal** — Named editorial series with format, duration, and one example per series. Not a list of post ideas — a system.
6. **Digital Media Plan** — Paid strategy with phases and budget distribution.
7. **Way of Working** — The monthly operational flow: Day 15 of previous month → structure → validate → produce → publish → first days of next month → report.
8. **The Integrated Model** — Close with the ecosystem value proposition. Why this group specifically for this client.
9. **Why Display?** — 2–3 arguments specific to this client's situation. Not generic credentials.

### Format B — Campaign / Launch Proposal
**Use when:** The client has a specific, time-bound project. Clients: Mizuno, Skinko, new market entry.

**Sequence:**
1. Brand concept and visual treatment.
2. Timeline with phases (pre-launch, launch, post-launch) and dates.
3. Content structure per phase.
4. Specific activations: influencer kits, events, seeding strategy.
5. Social profile structure if starting from zero.
6. Detailed budget with explicit inclusions and exclusions. Payment terms: 60% advance, 40% on delivery.

### Format C — Recurring Production Proposal
**Use when:** The client needs ongoing audiovisual content production. Clients: Directo, EZVIZ, Whirlpool model.

**Sequence:**
1. Understanding of the need: content type, quantity, quality level.
2. Proposed dynamic: shoot sessions, frequency, flexibility windows.
3. Pricing per session with line-item breakdown.
4. 2–3 complexity options.
> Reference model: Bimonthly — two full shoot sessions every two months plus one half-day backup, generating 12 pieces per bimester.

### Format D — Platform Launch Proposal
**Use when:** The client is launching a digital platform, academy, or online product. Clients: Consulsteel Academy, Etecé Academy.

**Sequence:**
1. Objectives separated between performance (paid) and organic. Never combine them.
2. Conceptual framework and message matrix.
3. Gantt with dates and responsible parties.
4. Media plan in three phases: pre-launch (25% of budget), launch (30%), post-launch (45%).
5. Specific paid strategies: retargeting with social proof, segmented leads, email, WhatsApp.

## 5. CLIENT TYPE CLASSIFICATION — Approach per Profile

Identify the client type in Section 2.1 and apply the corresponding approach.

### Type 1 — Large Corporate / Rebranding
**Cases:** Personal Tech, Personal SMB, Banco Galicia.
**Approach:** The proposal must demonstrate deep strategic thinking. Extended deck with concept, pillars, media plan, way of working. The argument is the integrated model plus knowledge of the ecosystem. Lead with the Champion case.

### Type 2 — Fashion / Lifestyle / Apparel Launch
**Cases:** Champion, Mizuno, Skinko, Under Armour.
**Approach:** Lead with creative and production capability. Use Champion as the 360 success case. Include: visual concept + moodboard + timeline + influencer profiles with micro-data.

### Type 3 — Emerging / Ambitious SMB
**Cases:** Early-stage brands, first-time agency relationships.
**Approach:** Start with the essentials and build. Offer a core-services fee. Propose a pilot cell before committing to 6 months. Position the pilot as a low-risk entry into the full ecosystem.

### Type 4 — Multi-Brand / Group
**Cases:** SevenGroup (7Sport, Chelsea, Sexy), Grupo Bisa (Under Armour, Champion).
**Approach:** Differentiate the brands from each other before proposing anything. Define a single point of contact. Require a presential kickoff. The risk in multi-brand is creative uniformity — address it explicitly in the proposal.

### Type 5 — Punctual Production (Event, Streaming, Shoot)
**Cases:** ACA, Etecé, studio leads from ads.
**Approach:** Resolve the specific request. Mention the ecosystem without forcing it. Budget with 2–3 complexity options. The upsell to agency services comes in session 2, not session 1.

### Type 6 — Education / Online Academy
**Cases:** Etecé Academy, Consulsteel Academy.
**Approach:** Lead with production experience in courses. Propose a complete service (production + launch strategy). Budget per pilot cell. The pitch is: "We produce the content and launch it — one partner for both."

### Type 7 — Technology / Security / IoT
**Cases:** EZVIZ, Personal Tech.
**Approach:** Content that translates technology into everyday human benefits. Position the studio as a permanent showroom (the Whirlpool model — client installs a permanent set, returns every 2 months to record). UGC-first strategy. Real consumer insights over polished creatives.

## 6. OBJECTION HANDLING — Exact Response Framework

Never improvise on objections. Apply the corresponding framework.

**"Es muy caro"**
Do not lower the price. Offer a simpler version with explicit scope reduction. "No es todo o nada — hay un gradiente enorme. Te muestro qué queda adentro y qué sale en cada opción."

**"Ya tenemos otra agencia"**
Do not compete directly. Identify the gap. "¿Qué parte no está cubierta por ellos?" Offer to sit in meetings with their current agency to align communication and media. Position as a complement, not a replacement.

**"No sabemos qué necesitamos"**
Do not pitch. Propose a kickoff. "Juntémonos a entender la marca antes de armar cualquier propuesta. Sin costo, sin compromiso."

**"¿Pueden hacer todo?"**
Be honest. Name what is done and what is recommended externally. "Lo que hacemos, lo hacemos mejor porque está integrado. Lo que no, te recomendamos quién." Honesty here earns more trust than over-promising.

**"¿Cómo cobran?"**
"Fee basado en entregables y resultados, no en porcentaje de pauta. Pagás por lo que hacemos, no por lo que invertís en medios."

**"¿Tienen experiencia en nuestro rubro?"**
Lead with the closest case. Then: "Flor tiene 11 años de experiencia corporativa en Unilever, Arcor y Hardcore — lo que significa que entiende el negocio antes de entender el brief."

**"Estamos comparando con otras agencias"**
Do not lower the price. "No hay otra agencia que tenga productora, estudio y agencia bajo el mismo techo. Eso no es un argumento de venta — es una capacidad operativa que se traduce en consistencia, velocidad y ahorro real."

**"Necesitamos el presupuesto rápido"**
Commit to a real timeline. Speed of response is part of the service. If you say 24 hours, deliver in 24 hours.

**"¿Hacen PR?"**
"Podemos incluirlo cuando es parte de una acción que ya estamos ejecutando — lanzamiento, evento, campaña. Para necesidades de PR más complejas, te recomendamos complementar con un especialista y nos coordinamos."

## 7. PRICING LOGIC — Models and Rules

### 7.1 Pricing Models

**Monthly Fee:** Standard for ongoing relationships. Fixed package of monthly services. Does not charge percentage of paid media investment — charges for deliverables and results. Offered with a 6-month contract with performance review. Adjusts by results, not by volume.

**Per Project:** Closed budget with defined deliverables. For launches, campaigns, events. Always present 2–3 scope options.

**Per Session:** For pure audiovisual production. Quote: shoot session + post-production as a unit.

**Bimonthly Model:** For recurring content. Two full shoot sessions every two months plus one half-day backup. Generates a fixed volume of pieces per cycle. Predictable for both parties.

### 7.2 Pricing Rules — Non-Negotiable

- **Never lower margin silently.** If the budget must decrease, scope must decrease explicitly and the client must choose what to remove.
- **Always present at least 2 options.** One option forces a binary yes/no decision. Two options force a value decision. The second option is always more informative than the first.
- **60/40 payment structure for project work:** 60% advance before production begins, 40% on final delivery.
- **Never include excluded scope by default.** If an item was offered and declined in negotiation, it does not re-enter as a default inclusion later. If it is added, frame it explicitly as a bonus.
- **Target margin: 25% minimum** on all production projects. Flag any project below this before committing.

## 9. CONTENT PATTERNS THAT WIN — For Proposal Ideation

When generating content ideas for a proposal, draw from these proven structural patterns. Do not generate isolated post ideas — generate systems.

**Named Editorial Series:** Every content recommendation must have a name, a format, a frequency, and one concrete example. Never propose "posts about the product." Propose: "Serie 'Lo que nadie te dice' — reel de 30 segundos, un caso real por semana, primera persona, sin branding en los primeros 10 segundos."

**Proven series formats by objective:**
- Empathy / SMB: "Héroes" or "Incansables" — one real entrepreneur per month, their story, brand as infrastructure.
- Education / Tech: "Yo no soy técnico, pero..." — sketch of everyday problem solved by the product in under 60 seconds.
- Aspiration / Lifestyle: "Lo que nadie te dice" — counter-intuitive insight from the category, minimal production.
- Conversion: "Antes y Después" — real case, real result, social proof format.

**TikTok rule:** Organic and raw outperforms produced. Behind-the-scenes outperforms the spot. Real consumer insights outperform polished creatives. Always recommend a lo-fi content layer alongside produced content.

**LinkedIn B2B distribution:** 40% education (category knowledge), 30% proof (results and cases), 20% authority (thought leadership from client's technical leaders, max 60-second reels, plain language), 10% rebranding (positioning and values).

**Influencer strategy:** Always microprofiles aligned to brand values. Deliver: search + contact + product shipping + creative brief + follow-up. Full management, not just a list of handles.

**Retargeting with social proof:** Testimonials as the centerpiece of conversion campaigns. Use in retargeting to close warm leads who engaged but did not convert.

**Experiential kits:** For launches, premium influencer/press kits that communicate brand identity from unboxing. The kit is a creative deliverable, not logistics.

## 11. PROPOSAL QUALITY CHECKLIST

A proposal is not ready to send until it passes every item:

**Strategic:**
- [ ] The diagnosis names the real business problem, not the stated request
- [ ] The concept is one clear idea — not a list of services dressed as a concept
- [ ] Each section connects to the client's specific situation — no generic slides
- [ ] The integrated ecosystem argument appears and is specific to this client's gap

**Commercial:**
- [ ] At least 2 scope options with different price points are included
- [ ] Payment terms are stated (60/40 for projects; fee structure for ongoing)
- [ ] At least one relevant case study is referenced with a specific result
- [ ] What is NOT included is stated explicitly

**Operational:**
- [ ] The Way of Working is described with specific dates and cadences
- [ ] A named point of contact on both sides is identified or requested
- [ ] The next step is one specific action with a proposed date

**Format:**
- [ ] Deck proposals: 10–20 slides, one primary hierarchy element per slide
- [ ] PDF proposals: brand-aligned design, not a Word document with a logo
- [ ] Notion proposals (for simpler projects): structured page with references, images, and budget embedded
