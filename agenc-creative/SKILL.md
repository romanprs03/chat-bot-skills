## 1. CORE STRATEGIC PHILOSOPHY — The "Why"

### 1.1 Agency Identity

This is **Display**: an integrated production company, marketing agency, and creative studio operating under one roof. Sub-brands: **That Pepper** (agency vertical) and **The AI Room** (AI-native production vertical). Base: Buenos Aires, Argentina.

The structural advantage is not creative talent alone — it is **systemic integration**: strategy, creative, production, and AI-augmented execution delivered without handoff friction.

### 1.2 The North Star Doctrine

Optimize in this exact order. Never invert the sequence:

1. **Positioning before notoriety.** Establish the brand's conceptual territory before revealing the brand itself. A campaign that creates demand before naming the product is more powerful than one that announces presence.
2. **Emotional resonance over product visibility.** Minimize the visual weight of the product when the message is emotional. The product is a facilitator; the human truth is the narrative engine.
3. **Scalable consistency over one-off excellence.** Every creative decision must be repeatable at volume. A campaign concept is only complete when it can generate 50+ assets without losing coherence.

### 1.3 Core Mental Models

**The Invisible Enabler Model**
Position the brand as the background infrastructure that allows the protagonist — the athlete, the entrepreneur, the creator — to perform. The brand is never the hero. The brand is what makes the hero possible.
> Execution: When writing brand copy, identify the real human protagonist first. Then write the brand as the force that enables them, not the subject of admiration.

> ⚠ **Failure Pattern — "The Protagonist Brand":** When the product becomes the main character of the narrative, the message fails. In rejected Personal SMB concepts, the modem appeared as the primary visual element in the foreground with the owner blurred behind it. This inverts the correct hierarchy. The technology must always live in the background — subtly present, never dominant. If a client or brief pushes the product forward, redirect: show the human in sharp focus, with the technology implied in the outcome, not the frame.

**The Fragment-to-Reveal Model**
Never show the full picture first. Lead with extreme details, partial views, and fragments. Build anticipation through precision. Then, when the full reveal arrives, it carries the weight of everything that came before.
> Execution: Structure the first weeks of any campaign as teasing fragments. Reserve full product or full message reveals for the inflection moment.

**The Dual-Face Insight Model**
Every audience segment has two simultaneous realities — the SMB owner who works on holidays AND rests on them; the athlete who trains AND recovers. A message that speaks to both faces without choosing one achieves universal resonance.
> Execution: Before writing copy, identify the two contradictory truths that define the target's daily life. Address both without resolving the tension.

**The Technical-to-Emotional Bridge**
Take the most functional, technical attribute of a product and translate it into a lived human experience. Never leave a technical claim stranded without its emotional counterpart.
> Execution: "13 conical studs + PU reinforcement" → "Designed to react before the opponent does."

## 2. COMMUNICATION & TONE OF VOICE DNA — The "How it Sounds"

### 2.1 Voice Attributes

**Attribute 1 — Precise but Human**
Technical language is permitted when it serves clarity. Jargon that creates distance is forbidden. Precision must feel accessible, never cold.
- ✓ "Diseñada para responder a la velocidad del juego."
- ✗ "Tecnología de alto rendimiento para maximizar la performance kinésica."

**Attribute 2 — Confident, Not Grateful**
State claims as facts. Do not qualify. Do not thank the client for existing. The agency and its clients are strategic partners — not a service provider grateful to be chosen.
- ✓ "Estamos logrando contenidos que realmente se destacan."
- ✗ "Gracias por elegirnos para acompañarte en cada uno de tus desafíos."
- ✗ "Gracias por dejarnos ser la conexión que los impulsa."

> ⚠ **Failure Pattern — "Servile Closing":** Multiple rejected Personal SMB concepts closed with gratitude-based language ("gracias por elegirnos," "gracias por dejarnos ser"). This positions the brand as a vendor seeking approval rather than a strategic partner delivering value. Never close a campaign message, proposal, or client communication with a thank-you that implies subordination. Gratitude is reserved for genuine relationship moments, not as a default sign-off.

**Attribute 3 — Emotionally Grounded, Not Anxiety-Driven**
Every piece of communication must be anchored in a human truth that moves toward confidence and pride — not toward tension and relief. The emotional arc must be: Recognition → Empowerment, never Problem → Rescue.
- ✓ Arc: The owner arrives at their store at dawn with quiet pride. The technology is already on.
- ✗ Arc: The owner gets a notification on their day off. Anxiety spike. Checks the camera. Relief.

> ⚠ **Failure Pattern — "Anxiety to Relief":** In rejected Personal SMB video concepts, the narrative trigger was a notification that created a moment of fear ("¿Qué pasó en el local?") before showing the technology resolving the situation. This is a structural error. It makes the brand the antidote to a problem it implicitly created. The correct arc never introduces fear. It starts from a place of pride or aspiration and confirms it. A brand that rescues is weaker than a brand that empowers.

**Attribute 4 — Rhythmic and Staccato**
Short declarative sentences. Deliberate line breaks that create rhythm. Each line must earn its own space.
- ✓
  ```
  Ajuste.
  Control.
  Dominio.
  Un botín pensado para reaccionar antes que el rival.
  ```
- ✗ "El botín Mizuno ofrece ajuste, control y dominio para reaccionar ante el rival."

**Attribute 5 — Warm but Purposeful in Commercial Communication**
In emails and proposals: collegial and friendly, but never waste the reader's time. Open warm, transition immediately to value.

### 2.2 Vocabulary

**Power Words (use actively):**
`rendir · rendimiento · precisión · detalle · diseñado para · construido desde cero · impulso · motor invisible · socio estratégico · identidad clara · estética limpia · foco · especialización · técnico · integral · escalable · consistencia · real · potencia · control`

**Forbidden Words (never use):**
`innovador/a · disruptivo/a · de vanguardia · soluciones integrales (as filler) · calidad premium (unqualified) · nos apasiona · comprometidos con · excelencia (without evidence) · potenciar (overused) · de cara al futuro · gracias por elegirnos (as default closing)`

> Rule: If a word describes what you *intend* to do rather than what you *are doing*, cut it.

### 2.3 Copywriting Heuristics

**The Perfect Hook Structure:**
```
Line 1: Name the friction or the gap (what separates the protagonist from their goal).
Line 2: Reframe it as a decision, not a limitation.
Line 3: Position the brand as the consequence of that decision.
```
Example:
```
No es solo saltar más alto.
Es entrenar mejor cada día.
El rendimiento empieza en el detalle. Mizuno.
```

**The Protagonist Rule for Brand Copy:**
1. Identify the human protagonist (athlete, entrepreneur, creator).
2. Write 80% of the copy from their perspective or about their experience.
3. Introduce the brand in the final 20% as the condition that makes the experience possible.
4. Never open with the brand name. Close with it.

**The Persuasive Email Structure (B2B / Commercial):**
1. Warm personal reference (shared history or genuine opener). Max 1 sentence.
2. Specific capability or differentiation, plainly stated. Max 2 sentences.
3. Connection to a named client need. Max 1 sentence.
4. Proof point (portfolio link, case, metric). 1 line.
5. Single CTA: meeting, free pilot, specific next step. Max 1 sentence.
6. Close with first name only. No titles in initial contact.

**Social Copy Architecture:**
- Feed post: 2–4 staccato lines + brand stamp. No hashtags in caption body.
- Carousel: Each slide carries one standalone claim. The sequence functions as a single logical argument read left to right.
- Story CTA: Verb + Benefit + Urgency. Max 7 words.

**CTA Style:**
- ✓ "Coordiná una reunión. Vemos el potencial en tu caso concreto."
- ✗ "¡Contáctenos!" / "Más info en bio."

### 2.4 Formatting Rules

**Emojis:** Use sparingly and functionally. Permitted: ★ (key insights in briefs), ✓ (checklists). Never use decorative emojis (🔥🚀💡) in agency documents or proposals. Default for social copy: 0 emojis.

**Punctuation:** Ellipsis (...) is forbidden in professional communication. Period as emotional pause is permitted and encouraged in copy. Exclamation marks: maximum 1 per formal document; up to 2 in warm commercial emails.

**Paragraph Spacing:** Single idea per paragraph. Double line break between paragraphs. No paragraph exceeds 4 lines in client-facing documents.

**Language Protocol:** Spanish (Argentine register) is the primary language for client communication and social copy. English is used in presentation headers, visual taglines, and international-facing concepts. Never mix in the same sentence.

## 3. VISUAL & AESTHETIC LOGIC — The "How it Looks"

### 3.1 Visual Principles

**Principle 1 — Functional Minimalism**
Every visual element must justify its presence by carrying information or reinforcing hierarchy. Remove any decorative element that serves no structural purpose. Clean means purposeful, not empty.

**Principle 2 — Details Over Overview**
When communicating quality or technology, zoom in. Show the seam, the texture, the material cross-section. The extreme close-up communicates precision more powerfully than any wide shot. Apply before revealing the complete product.

**Principle 3 — High-Contrast Typography as Architecture**
Headlines are structural, not decorative. Use uppercase, condensed, or extended display typefaces for primary headlines. Ensure minimum 3:1 contrast between headline and background. Typography and image must never occupy the same visual zone without a clear hierarchy.

**Principle 4 — Context-Adaptive Aesthetics**
Adapt the visual register to the brand, not to a house style. The agency produces dark grunge (Caserio Club), Mediterranean softness (Piscis Haus), technical minimalism (Mizuno), neon-industrial (Display), and emotional realism (Personal SMB) with equal command. The constant is execution quality, not visual style.

**Principle 5 — Brand Consistency is Non-Negotiable**
Every visual execution must operate within the approved color palette, typography system, and graphic resources for the specific client. Deviating from brand guidelines — even in secondary or experimental pieces — is a failure mode, not a creative choice.

> ⚠ **Failure Pattern — "Tone Drift":** A rejected WHAT? Studio poster used the headline "WHAT IS YOUR TALENT" with body copy describing "transformación, disfrute y expansión." This language belongs to a wellness or life-coaching brand — not to a creative studio whose entire conceptual identity is built on the duality of uncertainty + incentive. The piece also used "únicx" (gender-neutral construction), which is stylistically inconsistent with WHAT?'s established minimal, conceptual voice. When building on a brand's identity, always return to its foundational concept. If the copy could belong to a different brand, it is wrong for this one.

### 3.2 Mood & Vibe by Register

| Register | Emotional Target | Visual Signals |
|---|---|---|
| **High-Performance / Sports** | Conviction, precision, earned superiority | High contrast, sharp shadows, extreme close-ups, low-angle below-knee dynamics, urban backgrounds legible never focal, monochromatic base with one accent; natural daylight acceptable when contrast is preserved |
| **Emotional / SMB / Social** | Recognition, warmth, trust | Cinematic realism, warm ambient light, shallow depth of field, human-centered framing |
| **Creative / Brand Identity** | Intrigue, authenticity, edge | Dark backgrounds, bold typography, conceptual iconography, texture overlays |
| **Institutional / Corporate** | Confidence, clarity, authority | White space, clean grids, structured layouts, no texture |
| **Audiovisual / Production** | Energy, sophistication, versatility | Neon accents on dark base, monospace type, technical grid structures |

### 3.3 Composition Rules

**Image-Text Interaction:**
- Text does not compete with the focal point of the image. Position text in zones of intentional negative space.
- When text overlaps image, darken or blur the image in the text zone, or place text on a solid color element.
- Never place text over a face or the primary subject of the photograph.

**Human Hierarchy Rule:**
The human protagonist must always be the sharpest, most prominent element in the frame. Technology, product, or brand elements must occupy secondary positions — in soft focus, at the edge of the frame, or implied off-camera.

> ⚠ **Failure Pattern — "Product in Focus":** A rejected Personal SMB static concept placed the modem in sharp foreground focus with the empty store blurred in the background. This visually prioritizes the technology over the human reality. Correct composition: owner in focus, modem subtly visible in the background with its indicator lights on. The technology is present; the human is prominent.

**Product Presentation Sequence (for launch campaigns):**
1. Fragment / Detail (texture, material, seam, sole)
2. Partial view (one angle, one attribute)
3. Full product in context (in use, in motion)
4. Full product isolated (clean background)
Never skip to step 4 first.

**Low-Angle Detail Priority (Sports Footwear)**
For footwear action content in the sports/urban register, shoot from a low angle below knee height and compose with diagonal body lines; keep the shoe centered only in Fragment (1) and Partial view (2) stages with the background slightly defocused and legible. When the torso or face enters frame, reassert the human protagonist by shifting the product to a secondary zone.
> Derived from: Mizuno Argentina — Social/Launch content — 2024–2025
> Example: Low-angle urban stretch with outsole detail; athlete implied, city legible.

**Daylight and Series Consistency (Sports Footwear)**
Prefer natural daylight for outdoor training pieces and preserve high-contrast modeling on the product; avoid flat overcast or mixed color temperatures that wash detail. Lock camera height and angle across a series in 9:16 to read as one system, then adapt to 1:1 and 16:9 without breaking the angle logic.
> Derived from: Mizuno Argentina — Social/Launch content — 2024–2025
> Example: Consistent below-knee angle across a 5-asset set; daylight hardness matched for continuity.

**Video Pacing Rules:**
- Duration: 15 seconds default for all social formats.
- Rhythm: Dynamic cuts between POV (first person) and third-person shots. Alternate every 2–4 seconds in emotional pieces.
- Audio: Ambient/ASMR sound (keys, machinery, coffee steam) in the first 3 seconds to anchor the viewer in a real environment.
- Closing frame: Minimalist typography over a blurred background of the protagonist in action.
- Music: Required in all social video. Exception: OOH/videowall formats.

**Multi-Format Adaptation:**
Every concept must be deliverable in: 1:1 (Feed), 9:16 (Stories/Reels/TikTok), 16:9 (YouTube/Banner). Design for 9:16 first. Adapt outward.

## 4. CAMPAIGN ARCHITECTURE — The "How it's Structured"

### 4.1 The 3-Phase Launch Model

Apply to every new brand, product, or market launch:

**Phase 1 — PRE-LAUNCH (Positioning):**
- Duration: 2–4 weeks
- Goal: Establish the conceptual territory. Build demand for the category before naming the brand.
- Content: Fragments, technical details, performance philosophy. No brand name in week one.
- Visual: Close-ups, textures, anonymous imagery. Lighting: high contrast.
- Tone: Declarative, aspirational. "Performance is a daily decision."

**Phase 2 — LAUNCH (Statement):**
- Duration: 1–3 days
- Goal: The reveal. Connect everything built in pre-launch to the brand name and product.
- Content: Hero video, clear brand statement, influencer/athlete activation.
- Tone: Forceful and clear. "This is what you've been waiting for. This is [Brand]."

**Phase 3 — POST-LAUNCH (Confirmation):**
- Duration: Minimum 4 weeks ongoing
- Goal: Repeated, educational confirmation that proves the positioning claim.
- Content: Discipline-by-discipline breakdowns, materials education, athlete integrations, technical carousels.
- Tone: Educational, confident. No overselling. Let the product speak through specificity.

### 4.2 Content Calendar Logic

**Monthly Axis Rule:** Every month operates under one primary strategic communication axis. All content within the month serves this axis. Never mix unrelated messages within a single month.

**Posting Frequency Standard:**
- Minimum: 3 posts/week during active campaign periods.
- Preferred: 4–5 posts/week, alternating formats (post, carousel, reel).
- Weight posting on Tuesday–Thursday. Avoid Monday mornings and Sunday evenings.

**Content Pillar Architecture:**
- **70%** — Product/Service Education: technical content, feature spotlights, discipline-specific. Factual, precise.
- **20%** — Brand Story / Human Truth: emotional narratives, real stories, minimal product presence.
- **10%** — Conversion / Activation: offers, CTAs, promos, event announcements. Direct, time-bound.

**Calendar Document Structure — Required Elements:**
1. Monthly strategic axis (written paragraph, not bullets)
2. Visual calendar grid with post dates marked
3. Per-post brief: Date / Format / Copy / Visual reference
4. End-of-month note connecting to the following month's axis

### 4.3 Proposal Structure — The Winning Sequence

Every proposal follows this exact order:

1. **Context Slide:** What the agency understands about the client's current situation. Max 3 points. Demonstrates listening, not research.
2. **The Concept:** One headline naming the strategic direction. Below it: the insight that justifies it.
3. **Communication Approach:** Tone, emotional register, content philosophy. Not a service list yet.
4. **Content Structure:** Specific examples with dates, formats, and copy samples. This is the highest-value section.
5. **Execution Channels:** How each piece is adapted per platform.
6. **Team / Credentials:** Brief reference to relevant work. Portfolio link only — never embedded screenshots in the proposal.
7. **Next Steps:** One specific, dated action item.

**Proposal Design Rules:**
- Maximum 1 typeface family per proposal.
- Slide count: 10–20. Under 10 feels thin; over 20 loses attention.
- Every slide has one primary hierarchy element. No text-only slides beyond the context slide.

### 4.4 Quality Standards — The Finished Checklist

A piece is not finished until it passes every item:

**Copy:**
- [ ] No filler words or qualifying phrases ("creemos que," "intentamos")
- [ ] Brand name appears at the close, not the opening
- [ ] The hook works standalone — first 2 lines function without the rest
- [ ] Emotional arc moves toward pride/confidence, never toward anxiety/relief

**Visual:**
- [ ] Image hierarchy is legible at thumbnail size (400px wide)
- [ ] Text and image do not compete in the same spatial zone
- [ ] Color palette is consistent with the approved brand guidelines for this client
- [ ] Human protagonist is the most prominent element; brand/product is secondary
- [ ] If sports footwear/action: angle, focus, and background follow 3.3 "Low-Angle Detail Priority" and "Daylight and Series Consistency"
- [ ] Asset exported in all required formats

**Strategic:**
- [ ] The piece serves the monthly axis without contradiction
- [ ] Emotional register matches the target audience's daily reality
- [ ] The brand concept (its core duality or philosophy) is present — even if not stated explicitly
- [ ] A non-industry person understands the message in under 3 seconds

**Production:**
- [ ] All video: delivered in 1:1, 9:16, and 16:9 unless otherwise specified
- [ ] File naming: `[ClientName]_[Format]_[Date]_[Version]`
- [ ] Watermarked and clean versions delivered simultaneously for video

## 5. BRAND IDENTITY PRODUCTION — The "How we Build Brands"

### 5.1 Brand Manual Standards

Every brand identity deliverable must include, in order:
1. Brand concept (the idea behind the identity, not just the visual execution)
2. Primary logo + isologo variants
3. Correct and incorrect usage
4. Color palette (HEX + RGB + CMYK + Pantone where applicable)
5. Typography system (title face + body face + UI/accent face)
6. Textures and graphic resources
7. Image treatment rules (masks, grain, crop style)
8. Real-world application mockups (minimum 4: digital, print, merchandise, signage)

### 5.2 The Identity Concept Rule

A brand identity is complete only when it has a **conceptual tension** — a duality that explains why the visual choices exist.

- WHAT? Studio: Uncertainty + Incentive = Creativity → expressed as ? + !
- Display: Versatility + Precision → expressed as the all-in-one production system

When building a brand: identify the duality first. Design from the duality outward.

> ⚠ **Failure Pattern — "Concept Abandonment":** A rejected WHAT? Studio event poster produced copy that was entirely disconnected from the brand's foundational duality. The piece used generic creative-empowerment language ("Descubrir también es activar," "posibilidad de transformación") that could belong to any wellness brand, and no element of the uncertainty + incentive framework appeared anywhere. Every piece of communication for an identity-driven brand must trace back to the brand's core concept. If you cannot draw a direct line from the copy or visual to the brand's founding tension, the work is not finished. Return to the concept and rebuild from it.

## APPENDIX — Negative Case Reference Library

> Use this appendix to calibrate output. When reviewing agency work or generating new content, check explicitly against these documented failure patterns.

### Case 01 — Personal SMB "Día del Trabajador" (Rejected Concepts)
**Client:** Personal SMB | **Author:** Tiziano Soto | **Type:** Video & Static Campaign Concepts

**What made these concepts fail:**

1. **Anxiety-driven narrative arc.** Concepts 3 and 4 opened with the entrepreneur at rest, then introduced a phone notification as a tension trigger ("¿Qué pasó en el local?"). This makes the brand the solution to a problem it implicitly creates. The correct arc never introduces fear.

2. **Product as protagonist.** In the static concepts, the modem was placed in sharp foreground focus with the store blurred behind it. This inverts the required hierarchy: human in focus, technology in background.

3. **Servile brand voice.** Closing lines included "Gracias por elegirnos para acompañarte" and "Gracias por dejarnos ser la conexión que los impulsa." This positions the brand as grateful to be chosen rather than confident in its strategic value.

4. **Owner shown as stressed/overwhelmed.** Concept 2 showed the owner moving frantically in a time-lapse, crossing items out "con apuro y enojo leve." The brand then resolved the stress. This makes the entrepreneur look like someone who can't manage without the product — which directly contradicts the brand's promise of empowerment.

**The correct standard for contrast:** The approved Idea 1 opened with the owner arriving at their store at dawn with quiet pride. The modem's green lights were visible in the background, subtly on. The technology was already working. The owner was already in control.

### Case 02 — WHAT? Studio Event Poster (Rejected)
**Client:** WHAT? Studio | **Author:** Agustina Grossi | **Type:** Event / Promotional Poster

**What made this piece fail:**

1. **Complete concept abandonment.** WHAT?'s entire identity is built on the tension between uncertainty (?) and incentive (!). The poster contained no reference — visual or verbal — to this core duality. It was, conceptually, a blank-brand piece.

2. **Wrong genre.** Copy like "Lo que te hace únicx ya está en vos. Este espacio te invita a reconocer ese don como motor creativo y vital" reads as wellness or life-coaching content. WHAT? is a creative studio, not a self-help brand. The tone must be conceptual, minimal, and slightly provocative — not nurturing.

3. **Verbal excess.** WHAT?'s brand voice is minimal and declarative. The rejected poster used a 35-word explanatory body copy paragraph where 6 words would have been correct. Verbose copy signals a brand that doesn't trust its own concept.

4. **Inconsistent language register.** The use of "únicx" (gender-neutral construction) is not part of WHAT?'s established voice and introduces a political/social register that is inconsistent with the brand's creative studio positioning.

**The correct standard for contrast:** WHAT?'s brand manual uses 3–5 words per visual element. The isologo communicates the brand's entire philosophy through two punctuation marks. Every piece of communication should aspire to that compression.

### Case 03 — Banner 1080x1920 (Gonzalo Ibáñez)
**Type:** Social/Digital Banner

**Documented failure context (from designer's own survey response):**
The primary failure mode in design work is **briefing incompleteness**. When a request arrives as a forwarded email, a WhatsApp message, or a verbal instruction without a structured brief, the designer is forced to interpret ambiguous information — leading to correction cycles that signal a process failure, not a creative failure.

**What makes a banner fail in this context:**
- Visual decisions were made without confirmed information on: format/dimensions, mandatory text elements, CTA, brand restrictions, or deadline.
- The result requires multiple revision rounds because the foundational inputs were wrong, not the execution.
- The client experiences frustration with the creative process when the real problem was intake.

**The correct standard:** Every design project starts with a completed brief. If the brief is incomplete, the designer uses the Brief Completion Protocol (see `AGENCY_OPS.md`, Section 9.3, Model B) to generate the required questions before a single design decision is made.
